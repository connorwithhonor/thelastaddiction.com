What Makes the Fight Rigged Instead of Just Hard?

Hard would be fine. Hard is push-ups. Hard is a 14 hour shift. Food addicts are not afraid of hard, we do hard every single day just living inside a body that wants what it wants.

Rigged is different. Rigged means the rules themselves are bent against you, quietly, in ways nobody explains at the weigh-in. In this fight there are 2 fixes in place at the same time. The first fix is inside you: your own biology treats the fat you lose as stolen property and works around the clock to steal it back. The second fix is outside you: the food on the shelf was engineered, on purpose, by very smart people, to slide past the shutoff valve in your brain that is supposed to say enough.

Either one alone would explain a lot of failed diets. Both together, running simultaneously, every day, for decades? That explains your entire adult history with the scale. So let me take them one at a time, with the receipts.

Why Does Your Body Fight to Get the Fat Back?

The polite term is adaptive thermogenesis. The honest translation: after you lose a large amount of weight, your body cuts its own energy bill below what a body your new size should burn. It is not that a 220 pound version of you burns less than the 350 pound version did. Of course it does, there is less of you. The rigged part is that the new 220 pound you burns less than a man who was always 220. Your body is running a deficit protest. It wants the stash back.

The cleanest proof anyone has ever produced came from the Biggest Loser contestants. Researchers followed them for 6 years after the show, long after the cameras and the trainers and the crying weigh-ins were gone. 6 years out, their metabolisms were still burning roughly 500 fewer calories a day than expected for their body size. Read that timeline again. Not 6 weeks. Not 6 months while the body recalibrated. 6 years, and the penalty was still being charged daily, even though most of them had already regained much of the weight.

Think about your body the way your great-grandparents thought about the root cellar. Fat is the winter stash. For almost all of human history, a raided stash meant somebody might not see spring. So the body keeps books like a paranoid farmer: the first big loss goes in the ledger, and the ledger never gets erased. It does not matter that you have a Costco membership and 3 supermarkets within 5 miles. The accounting system predates supermarkets by about 300,000 years. Your body never forgives the first loss. It just waits, burns less, whispers more, and stalks the refill.

So when maintenance felt harder than the diet, when you swore you were eating like a normal person and gaining anyway, you were not imagining it and you were not lying to yourself. You were paying a 500 calorie daily tax that nobody at the gym ever told you existed.

Is the Food Really Engineered to Beat Your Fullness Signal?

Now the second opponent, the one waiting outside your body. For years the food industry's defense was simple: our products have the same calories, the same fat, the same sugar as anything you could cook at home, so if you overeat them, that is on you. It sounded airtight. Then somebody finally tested it properly.

In the landmark trial, researchers locked volunteers into an inpatient ward and fed them 2 diets, weeks apart. One was ultra-processed, one was unprocessed, and here is the part that matters: the 2 menus were matched for calories, sugar, fat, salt, and fiber. Same macros on paper. Eat as much or as little as you want. If the industry defense were true, both arms would eat about the same.

They did not. On the ultra-processed diet, the same people ate roughly 500 calories a day more. Nobody told them to. They did not report feeling hungrier. They just ate faster and further, meal after meal, and the weight moved in exactly the direction you would predict. Same humans, same willpower, same macros. The only variable was the engineering.

That is what modern food is for. It is formulated to hit the bliss point and clear your satiety signal like a runner beating a throw to the plate. The signal is not broken. The signal is slow, and the food was built to be faster. I have written on this site about how the fat was never the real problem, and about the tobacco playbook that built the snack aisle. This study is the mechanism in a lab coat: 500 extra calories a day, installed at the factory.

How Big Is the Swing You Have Been Fighting Against?

Do the arithmetic your diet coach never did. Your body holds back roughly 500 calories a day of burn after a major loss, and holds that grudge for years. The food supply pushes you roughly 500 calories a day past full without your permission or your awareness. That is a combined swing in the neighborhood of 1,000 calories a day, running against you, every day, before you have made a single choice.

You brought willpower to a 1,000 calorie ambush. Then you called yourself weak for losing.

1,000 calories a day is not a character flaw. It is not laziness, it is not gluttony, it is not a lack of grit. It is a metabolic penalty on one side and industrial design on the other, and you have been standing in the middle taking the blame for both. Every failed diet you have ever cried about in a parked car makes a different kind of sense once you see the size of the swing. You were not losing to a donut. You were losing to a system.

How Did I Lose 135 Pounds Inside a Rigged Fight?

I stopped playing the game on its home field. That is the whole strategic insight, and it took me most of my adult life and a few hundred lost rounds to see it.

Every diet I ever failed had one thing in common: I kept eating while I did it. Smaller portions, cleaner macros, better intentions, but the transaction itself continued. And as long as the transaction continues, both fixes stay live. The engineered food keeps getting its at-bats against my slow fullness signal, and every reduced-calorie day feeds the adaptive thermogenesis spiral, the same starve-and-suppress program I broke down in Caloric Restriction Is Not Fasting. Eating less of the rigged game is still playing the rigged game.

Fasting steps completely outside the food. When I am not eating, the bliss point has no mouth to work on. The 10 PM drive-thru has no transaction to complete. The engineered crunch, the salt-fat-sugar spike, the eat-faster-than-the-signal design, all of it needs one thing from me to function, and that thing is a bite. No bite, no hijack. For the hours and days I am fasted, the second opponent is not weakened. It is unemployed.

That is how 135 pounds came off in 7.5 months. No Ozempic. No Zepbound. No surgeon. Not because I am tougher than you, and I need you to hear that part straight: I am not. I had failed with every eat-less strategy invented since the 1980s. Fasting was not a harder version of dieting for me. It was a different door out of a burning building, and walking through it was easier than standing in the fire negotiating with the flames.

Did Fasting Solve My Food Addiction?

No. And I would be one more liar selling you one more before-and-after if I let you believe otherwise.

I have not solved this. I fight it constantly. There are days the fight is background noise and days it is the loudest thing in the room. If you asked me for the clinical truth about where I stand, I would tell you I am functionally dependent on fighting it, the way a sober alcoholic is dependent on the daily practice of not drinking. The war did not end when the scale said 135 down. The war got a better-armed soldier, that is all.

Here is the part that keeps me writing this site: the more I learn, the easier it gets. Every mechanism I can name is a punch I can see coming. Knowing about the 500 calorie metabolic grudge means a stall on the scale reads as physics instead of personal failure. Knowing the food is engineered to outrun my fullness signal means the first bite of the engineered stuff is a decision I make with open eyes or refuse to make at all. The fight stays rigged. But a rigged fight you can see is survivable, and some days it is even winnable on points.

Do the GLP-1 Shots Fix the Rigged Fight?

Straight answer, because this room does not do tribal warfare over medicine: the shots are real, and what they actually do is put back the brake the food removed.

GLP-1 drugs work largely by faking the fullness signal, the same signal the ultra-processed supply was engineered to blow past. The food industry spent decades building products that beat the brake, and now the pharmaceutical industry sells a brake that beats the food, and yes, both of them get paid either way. But mechanically, for the person taking it, the shot restores something that was taken from you. Appetite goes quiet. The 500 calorie ambush from the outside opponent gets smaller. That is not cheating. That is un-rigging one side of the fight with a prescription.

What the shot does not touch, in my experience and in the stories that come through this site every week, is the ritual. The pull into the parking lot. The unwrapping. The eating in the car with the radio on so the silence does not ask questions. I wrote about that gap in The Fifty Dollar Shot and the Ritual, along with the numbers that are not on the label, and every word of it still stands now that the floodgates are open.

So here is where I land, and it is the same place I always land. The shot is one door. Fasting is another. Both of them are exits from the rigged casino, and arguing about which exit is more honorable while people are still trapped inside is a luxury for people who have never been trapped. I walked through the fasting door and I can tell you exactly what it cost and exactly what it paid. If you are looking at the shot door, that is a medical decision with real tradeoffs, and the person to walk through it with is your doctor, not an influencer and not me.

What Did the 2026 Dietary Guidelines Finally Admit?

Something happened this year that I honestly did not expect to see: the new US dietary guidelines explicitly tell Americans to eat less ultra-processed food. First time. Ever.

Understand what a reversal that is. Through every food pyramid and every MyPlate refresh, the official guidance talked nutrients and food groups and portions while refusing to name the category. You could follow the old rules to the letter, hit your grains and your low-fat dairy, and do it entirely with products engineered to make you eat 500 calories past full, and the guidelines had nothing to say about it. The playbook would tell you how much to eat and stay silent about the fact that the food itself was designed to make the how-much impossible to hold.

Now the opponent has a name in the official record. That does not fix the shelf. The engineered products did not get reformulated overnight, and the companies that make them will fight the language in every revision cycle from here to forever. But for you, one addict reading this in the middle of your own rigged fight, it is a form of vindication with a government seal on it. The thing you kept losing to is real enough that the guidelines finally pointed at it. You were not crazy, and you were never just weak.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is adaptive thermogenesis in plain language?

It is your body cutting its own energy bill after you lose weight, below what a body your new size should burn. Lose a large amount of fat and your metabolism does not just settle at the level of a naturally smaller person. It drops under that line, on purpose, because your body reads the lost fat as a raided winter stash it intends to refill. That suppression is why maintenance feels harder than the diet did.

What did the Biggest Loser study actually find?

Researchers followed contestants for 6 years after the show and found their metabolisms were still burning roughly 500 fewer calories a day than expected for their size. Not during the weight loss. 6 years later. Most had regained much of the weight, and the metabolic penalty stayed anyway. It is the clearest evidence on record that the body defends its fat long after the diet ends.

Why do I overeat ultra-processed food without meaning to?

Because it is built for exactly that. In a landmark inpatient trial, people were fed an ultra-processed diet and an unprocessed diet matched for calories, sugar, fat, salt, and fiber on the menu. On the ultra-processed arm they ate about 500 more calories a day without being told to eat more and without reporting more hunger. The food is engineered to move past your fullness signal before the signal can fire. That is a design outcome, not a personal failure.

Do GLP-1 drugs like Ozempic fix food addiction?

They restore a brake, and that is real. The shots work largely by faking the fullness signal that engineered food was built to blow past, which is why appetite goes quiet on them. In my experience the addiction is bigger than appetite. The ritual, the cues, the 10 PM drive-thru pull, those are not silenced by a fullness signal. The shot is one legitimate door and fasting is another. I walked through the fasting door. If you are weighing the medication, take that decision to your doctor, not to a blog, including this one.

Is fasting better than Ozempic or Zepbound?

I will not rank them for you, because this is personal experience and not medical advice. What I can report: I lost 135 pounds in 7.5 months with extended fasting and no GLP-1 drug, and fasting worked for me because it steps completely outside the food. Engineered food cannot hijack a mouth that is not eating. The drugs quiet the signal while you keep eating. Fasting removes the transaction entirely for a stretch of time. Different tools, different doors. Talk to your physician before attempting either.

What do the 2026 US dietary guidelines say about ultra-processed food?

For the first time, the guidelines explicitly tell Americans to eat less ultra-processed food. Every previous edition talked around it with nutrients, food groups, and portion advice while never naming the category doing the damage. Naming it matters because you cannot fight an opponent the official playbook refuses to admit exists. It took until 2026 for the government's own guidance to point at the engineered food supply directly.
