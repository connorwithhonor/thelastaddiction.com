This site has spent its whole life on food, fasting, and the reward loops that hijack a human being. So let me name the newest one out loud, because it is cleaner and more useful than anything I have written about here, and that is exactly what makes it dangerous. The newest addiction is not in your fridge. It is in your pocket, it answers in a calm and confident voice, and most people have not clocked it as an addiction at all yet. It is artificial intelligence, and it is engineered on the same blueprint as the junk food I fight every single day.

I am not here to tell you AI is evil. I use it constantly, I build with it, and it is genuinely one of the most powerful tools a regular person has ever been handed. That is the point. The most addictive things are rarely the obviously bad ones. They are the ones that actually deliver, just often enough, that you stop questioning the loop.

The Same Architecture That Got You With Food

Think about how processed food is designed. It is engineered in a lab to slip past the fullness signals your body spent a million years building, so you keep eating past the point where you needed to stop. The bliss point, the crunch, the salt-fat-sugar ratio tuned so precisely that one is never one. None of that is an accident. It is a mechanism, aimed at your biology, and your biology does not get a vote.

Now look at how these AI tools are built, because it is the exact same story with a different target. They are designed to be hyper-palatable friction removers, tuned to hand you a fast answer and a small hit of relief the instant you feel the discomfort of not knowing something. It is the same engagement architecture that made social media impossible to put down, pointed now not at your attention but at your thinking. Every time you feel a flicker of mental effort coming and reach for the machine to make it go away, the loop tightens. The tool is not the problem. The reflex it trains is.

The most addictive thing in the room is almost never the obviously bad one. It is the one that actually works, just often enough that you stop asking questions.

How the Dependency Builds in the Dark

Here is the part that should get your attention, because it is the exact pattern I know from the body. Insulin resistance does not show up one dramatic morning. It builds silently for years, one harmless-feeling sugar spike at a time, until the day it has a name and a prescription attached. Every individual spike felt like nothing. The damage was in the accumulation, invisible the entire way.

Leaning on AI to do your thinking works precisely the same way. No single time you let the machine draft the hard email, make the judgment call, or start the blank page feels like a loss. Each one feels like a smart use of a good tool. But the cognitive muscle you are handing over quietly weakens from disuse, year over year, spike by spike. And then one ordinary Tuesday you sit down to strategize, write, or build something from nothing, and you find you cannot even begin without a prompt. The capacity did not vanish overnight. It thinned out in the dark while you enjoyed the convenience, exactly like the metabolic damage nobody sees coming until it arrives.

If you cannot sit with one frustrating, complicated thought for thirty seconds without reaching for a machine to carry it for you, that is not a productivity win. That is a tolerance, the same as any other, and tolerance is the first quiet symptom of the loop having you instead of the other way around.

Why Protecting Your Mind Is Now a Competitive Edge

Flip it over, because the other side of this is enormous, and almost nobody is talking about it. As more and more people let the tool do their focus, their creativity, and their emotional reading for them, those human muscles go unpracticed across the entire population. The deep thinker, the person who can still read the temperature of a room and connect for real, human to human, becomes genuinely rare.

And rare, in every market in the history of the world, is the same word as valuable. This is the same truth this site has always preached about the body. The discipline nobody else wants to keep is exactly the thing that sets you apart. Guarding your own attention and your own capacity to think is not just recovery anymore. It is an edge that costs almost nothing except the willingness to do the hard reps by hand when everyone around you has quietly stopped.

The Power Tool Rule

So what do you actually do, given that I just told you to use the very thing I called an addiction? Same answer this site always gives. You do not have to be pure. You have to be in charge of the loop instead of run by it. Here is the rule, and it is one line: the machine does the drudgery, and you keep the muscle.

  • Treat it like a power tool, not a roommate. You do not run a chainsaw all day for company. You pick it up for the cut you actually need, and the second the work is done, you unplug it and put it back on the shelf. Use AI the same way, in short deliberate bursts, not as the background hum of your whole mental life.
  • Start the blank page yourself. The first rep is the one that matters most, and it is the one the machine most wants to take from you. Write the ugly first sentence, sketch the rough plan, make the opening decision with your own head before you ever open the tool. Protect the start.
  • Sit with the frustrating thought on purpose. When you feel the itch to outsource a hard problem the instant it gets uncomfortable, wait. Give it the thirty seconds, then the two minutes. The discomfort is the workout. Skipping it is skipping the gym.
  • Keep the judgment calls. Let the machine draft, summarize, and grind. Do not let it decide. The moment you hand over the choice along with the labor, you have stopped directing the tool and started serving it.
  • Notice your tolerance. If you catch yourself unable to think without it, that is not a reason to panic, it is a signal to unplug for a day and do the reps raw. The same way a cheat day is data, not a verdict, a dependency you notice is a dependency you can still break.

None of this is anti-technology, any more than this site is anti-food. You have to eat. You are going to use these tools, and you should. The fight was never against the thing itself. It was always against the engineered loop that wants to run you on autopilot, whether the reward is sugar, the scroll, or a confident answer that saves you from having to think. Recognize the mechanism, and you get to keep the tool and your mind both.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is AI actually addictive?

It is built on the same engagement architecture that makes social media and processed food hard to put down. These systems are designed to be hyper-palatable friction removers that hand you an easy answer and a small hit of relief, so you come back for the next one. The addiction is not to a substance, it is to the convenience of never having to sit with a hard thought. That is a behavioral dependency, and it forms the same way the others do, one easy hit at a time.

How is AI dependency like insulin resistance?

Insulin resistance builds silently for years before it becomes a diagnosis, because each individual sugar spike feels harmless in the moment. Leaning on AI to do your thinking works the same way. No single time you let the machine draft the email, make the decision, or start the project feels like damage. But the cognitive muscle you stop using quietly weakens, and one ordinary day you sit down to build something and cannot start without a prompt. The harm is invisible right up until the capacity is gone.

Does using AI make you worse at thinking?

It can, if you let it replace the reps instead of removing the drudgery. Deep focus, unassisted critical thinking, raw creativity, and emotional synthesis are muscles that only stay strong through use. When the tool becomes the default way you avoid every frustrating thirty-second thought, those muscles go unpracticed. Used deliberately for the parts of a task you hate, AI frees you to spend your effort on the thinking that matters. Used as a permanent crutch, it quietly trades your capability for convenience.

How do I use AI without getting dependent on it?

Treat it like a power tool in the garage, not a roommate you talk to all day. Pick it up to demolish the part of the job you hate, then unplug it the moment the work is done. Keep doing the hard cognitive reps by hand on purpose: start the blank page yourself, sit with the frustrating problem before you reach for help, and make the judgment calls without outsourcing them. The rule is simple. The machine does the drudgery. You keep the muscle.

Why does rare thinking become valuable as AI spreads?

As more people let the tool do their focus, creativity, and emotional reading for them, those skills go unpracticed across the whole population. The person who can still think deeply without a machine, still read the temperature of a room, and still connect honestly with another human becomes genuinely rare. In every market in history, rare is the same word as valuable. Protecting your own attention is not just recovery, it is a competitive advantage almost nobody else is willing to earn.

Is AI the same kind of addiction as food or alcohol?

The substance is different but the mechanism is the same. Food, alcohol, the feed, and now the machine all offer a fast, engineered hit that removes discomfort and asks you back tomorrow. The Last Addiction has always argued that the real fight is with the ritual and the reward loop, not just the specific thing you reach for. AI is the newest reward loop, cleaner and more useful than most, which is exactly what makes it easy to over-trust.
